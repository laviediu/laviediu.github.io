I wrote the following entry on May 31st. I wanted to understand someone I deeply care about. I was wondering, what I did not understand that my words hurt in the way I failed to convey. I thought it was a choice, when it is a struggle. I ordered Dr. Lang's third book, titled *Losing My Religion: A Call For Help*, on April 19th. It is in my hand today, finally. It was supposed to be delivered on May. However, presumably, due to the America-Iran war, the book got lost during the shipping process. I contacted the seller, and they offered a refund. I asked whether they still have the book in stock, and they found it in their New York office. At the time, I was just hoping that the book won't get lost again, that the seller truly sent it, that I could read the book for however long it will arrive. My flight to Seoul was on June 22nd, and the book wasn't delivered yet. At that point, I was just telling myself, "If the book is meant to arrive in my hand, it will come". Today, it is July 12th, right after my arrival from Narita airport, I checked my mailbox, and it was there! I was so happy that I forgot I should have been happier for finally being home, haha. I opened the book and quickly went through the contents. There is a poem, I thought, "Finally, a language that I understand best". I read it and I finally understand the struggle. Of course, I couldn't stop myself from crying. At this point, everyone knows that I am *that person*, someone who cries just because of a sunset. Ah, my poor heart.<br>
<br>
Here is what I wrote on May 31st, my own struggle.<br>
<br>
*My eyes are apparently tired from crying, and since today should be the beginning of my follicular phase, I can think a little bit more clearly. I stumbled upon a youtube recommendation of a case where someone wrote a book, after collecting evidence for 20 years, about the rational deduction of a Necessary Entity (NE). Before that, I want to provide some personal historical contexts.*<br>
<br>
*I was born Muslim. It was a culture. The rituals were habits I have been accustomized to for years, because Mom would ask me whenever I didn't do the rituals. I began to search as I grow older and live outside my parents' house. Contrary to what I expected, the search led me closer to what was taught instead. There was period when the rituals were soul-less, it was inconvenience. There was also period when the rituals made me feel ashamed, because I became the inconvenience.*<br>
<br>
*Being born Muslim is a privilege for some people, and I did realize it later. However, there was also a period when I wish I found this religion by myself. Later, I have come to a realization that choosing this religion or continuously practicing, after being presented with other arguments and experiencing life outside what it taught, means that I found it by myself. Because personally, it is insulting to be regarded as a lazy thinker, as blindly following something out of circumstances I was born into. And it would have meant that I am agreeing to what people generally see as an utter delusion.*<br>
<br>
*While rational thinking always holds true and indisputable, it is not the major part of how I see the world. Or I should say thinking from the root of everything, it is absurd to regard how I think as irrationality when I practice rational thinking daily for my PhD research. Although again, I develop gut feeling on several parameters I believe would increase the performance metrics, that would be intuitive thinking from past experiences, or rational thinking based on past evidence. Ah language is losing me. Rather than defending how I normally think, let's just reduce myself as an irrational thinker for the sake of easier discussion.*<br>
<br>
*I am used to view the world as a series of implications. Hence, a substantial reason of why I finally chose this religion is because it has the best accountability explanations. In the past, I watched a lot of theistic-atheistic debates, in which arguments are presented by experts (or I would rather say, people who possess specialized knowledge and/or courage) from each side. One thing I recognize after the debates is that it was all about winning or losing, not understanding. Rather than satisfaction, I often found myself being flabbergasted. However, I still watch the US political debates occasionally, because most of the times they are far more hilarous than Trevor Noah's standup comedy.*<br>
<br>
*I read the first chapter of the book, where it asks the necessity of something incontingent. The book is titled "The Divine for Critical Minds". I said that I love someone. And to love a person, or any being for that matter, means to show my love and act on it. Therefore, this is an attempt to understand the person I love. Although we arrived on different conclusions, I am not someone who easily rejects different perspectives, especially from someone I love.*<br>
<br>
*Since my religion places inquiries as a necessary exercise for belief in its scripture, i.e. the God that I believe is literally asking "do you not think?" repeatedly, then rational thinking should lead me closer to the truth. And since the language of the scripture is a revelation, not a persuasion, a rational thinking should ideally proves it. Although, I will not deny that I am biased. The author of the book specifically address several people, except those with baggages from past experiences and those allergic to religions. It is addressed to those who deem sense experience the only reliable source of knowledge (this would partly be my own self), those who do not see God as a necessary explanation, those who have not challenged their own framework strongly (again, it would be my own self), and those who have accepted the views of people believed to be smarter than themselves.*<br>
<br>
*Bring it on, God. I am starting the challenge.*<br>
<br>
*Alright, since it is not an easy read (i.e. my mind is still under a heartbreak effect), I will re-read the first chapter to fully grasp what the author meant. I found several essential things, though, and I quote in the following.*<br>
<br>
*"Similarly, if intelligence cannot be reliably used by intelligent beings to investigate God then it means thinking about God's existence is an exercise in futility."*<br>
<br>
*"The universe comes from nothing, because a law like gravity is responsible for the universe. For some reason this particular fallacy seems to be a favorite of physicists."*<br>
<br>
*"If some formula or final theory is developed that can account for every detail of our universe, including the laws along with all their constants, "the next question would be: where did it come from? And why did it exist in the first place? The answer would probably be a superformula. But where did that come from?"."*<br>
<br>
*The law-of-physics-question way of thinking above is how I came to conclusion with my "irrational" thinking in the past.*<br>
<br>
*"It is logically possible that an NE works through mechanisms such as the cause and effect that we find throughout our universe and still remain a Necessary Entity. That concept of an NE only explains the cause. Formulas of how the universe may have unfolded is discussing something entirely different: it is talking about the process. If we happen to figure out the process by which the universe came about, we did not solve the question of the original cause of the universe."*<br>
<br>
*"If we doubt logic, we eliminate the common ground that we all stand on. So someone who argues against rational thinking is essentially saying that discussion means little because there is no way to know anything."*<br>
<br>
*That very sentence above, huh, it would be me. I was being utterly ridiculous, as it turned out. The following one sentence is the book quoting another work.*<br>
<br>
*"The aim of science is to explain phenomena and predict new observations. Practicing scientific research means to find out why an effect has been produced. It would be ridiculous and absurd to answer that there is no reason or no cause leading to the observed results--that results simply happen like that."*<br>
<br>
*"A contingent thing cannot explain itself. It can only be explained by something incontingent."*<br>
<br>
*"The one popularized by Professor Dawkins, originally mentioned in his "The God Delusion", is that the concept of the Necessary Entity is not a valid explanation because the Necessary Entity is more organized and complex than the things designed, such as the universe. That makes the Necessary Entity more improbable. This line of reasoning may have been correct if being complex did not mean being contingent."*<br>
<br>
*"Believing in a God that could appear in time and space would also mean that this Being has an attribute of contingency and so is contingent itself."*<br>
<br>
*"If God is necessarily as God is, then God's choices are necessarily as they are, and the freedom of choice evaporates."*<br>
<br>
*"Whether or not that Necessary Being must act necessarily is a separate question, the answer to which does not follow merely from the necessity of His existence. God's choice, or will, is secondary to the necessity of His existence.*<br>
<br>
*"Being logical is being reasonable. There is no way of knowing what the truth of any matter is if it can only be found through thinking irrationally."*<br>
<br>
*"Logic, however, is only one piece of the puzzle. People further say that they "need other sources of information" to support the case for God. That is a fair request."*<br>
<br>
*"The case for a Necessary Entity is equal to--if not stronger than--the other possibilities."*<br>
<br>
<br>
Those were what I wrote at the time. If it was a struggle, and I failed to understand the struggle as a choice instead, then my words were as sharp as a hundred papercuts. Understanding, as it turned out, is a very difficult thing to do. We can understand how something comes about, but could we truly understand and feel how our actions were perceived at the other end? The following poem helps me to understand and feel the struggle. It is impossible to experience the depth of what other people feel, but it is not impossible to reach even a tiny fraction of that feeling. Here is the poem. It is titled *Torn Between Two Worlds* by Carli Caris.<br>
<br>
*Torn between two worlds<br>
Searching for identity<br>
On a quest for solitude<br>
A place where I can be me.*<br>
<br>
*I look in the mirror<br>
But who is staring back at me?<br>
I feel like an innocent girl<br>
Who has lost her virginity.*<br>
<br>
*I look at the people<br>
Staring and whispering as I pass by<br>
Calling me a "traitor"<br>
Believing that my faith is a lie.*<br>
<br>
*I search deep inside my soul<br>
Questioning the inner me<br>
Praying for my struggle<br>
For peace and solidarity.*<br>
<br>
*I muster up the courage<br>
To speak about who I am<br>
Reflecting on where I've been<br>
Proud to take this stand.*<br>
<br>
*I'm torn between two worlds<br>
Not fitting into one or the other<br>
I've adopted to complement both<br>
Without feeling smothered.*<br>
<br>
*People judge before I speak<br>
They criticize and analyze<br>
Trying to make me weak<br>
"A slave I am not," I cry.*<br>
<br>
*I slowly walk through the doors<br>
Preparing my spirit and soul<br>
Knowing that no matter what they say<br>
I am still completely whole.*<br>
<br>
*I don't fit here, nor do I fit there<br>
I'm somewhere in between<br>
Identity could have been lost<br>
But in the end I have to be me.*<br>
