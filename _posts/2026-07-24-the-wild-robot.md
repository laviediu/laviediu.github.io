I watched *The Wild Robot* last weekend, it was such a good movie. The robot was stranded in a forest, looking for its first task. The female-voiced robot finally found a hatching egg, a baby gosling, after offering help to every animal she met. Little did she know, she became a mom! She met a mama rat and her children (whose names she can't remember), very cute babies, they pretend to die during crisis hahaha. I captured the hilarious conversations between her and the mama rat.<br>
<br>
The Robot: "I do not have the programming to be a mother."<br>
Mama Rat: "No one does."<br>
The Robot: "We just make it up."<br>
<br>
It reminds me of a TV series, titled *Workin' Moms*, I watched several months ago when I was trying to understand motherhood. While it was not exactly the kind that I was hoping to watch but it was both entertaining and teaching me things I didn't know.<br>
<br>
*No one knows how to be a good mother.*<br>
<br>
But it doesn't mean that I can't learn, it means that my knowledge is limited. Here is also the inconvenient truth, I can't protect them from getting hurt through my mistakes. Perhaps because it is an Asian culture, apologies are not something commonly practiced in Asian households. From what I remember, I think Mom only began to say sorry later when I was much older. Back then, Mom often threw ultimatums whenever I couldn't stop crying. I remember one time she said that she would go abroad if I kept crying hahaha, I am sorry Mom. To be fair, I cried very easily, and a lot. I still do and I can't *fix* it, I accept it now. Mom also used to say that I shouldn't cry, that I need to be strong. Over time, she changes her stance regarding crying though. Perhaps she realized that crying is not equal to being weak.<br>
<br>
My friends and I were discussing about a case. One of us asked, "What would you say to your kid regarding fights in school?". One told us, "I will tell my kid to fight back and report the fight to the teacher." Another one said, "I am not sure." It was actually quite foreign for me because I have never experienced bullying at school, nor did I saw one. I replied, "I am not sure whether this is the correct way, but I think I will tell my kid to say that it hurts. If they do it again, I will tell my kid to report to the teacher."<br>
<br>
I later realize that motherhood is not about having the right answer, but about growing with the child. From my perspective, I don't know the reason behind another kid hitting my hypothetical kid, but I think expressing inconvenience is the first step and that having the benefit of the doubt is a necessary education. If the other kid still did the same thing twice, then my kid has the right to report to the teacher. It is also teaching my kid to seek help and let the authority handle the conflict when conflict exceeds what they can handle. I could say to my kid that he/she should fight back, but then I will teach values that I don't want my kid to adopt. It is the value of *an eye for an eye*, and justifying a revenge.<br>
<br>
Conversely, what if my hypothetical kid is the one who hits other kids? I wish he/she won't, but if my kid does that, I will ask him first to apologize. I will then ask the reasoning. By apologizing first without knowing the reasoning, it will teach my kid that abuse of any kinds is unacceptable. If he turns out to be the one being wronged, I will apologize to my kid. However, even this latter method means that I didn't give the benefit of the doubt to my kid. And yet, I think everything varies case by case. Everything seems to be nuanced. And the question is, how do I educate my kid?<br>
<br>
I think the less distant question will be, how do I know whether I am ready to be a wife? How do I know whether I am ready to hold the responsibility?<br>
<br>
Lately I have been asking myself the role difference between a husband and a wife. My parents are quite unconventional, in a sense. Dad supports me throughout my education, but Dad earns for himself. Mom doesn't really tell Dad when she is struggling. Dad doesn't seem to mind her working without proper rest. It breaks my heart a little.<br>
<br>
If I look further into Islamic examples from the life of The Prophet, the role of a husband is very nuanced as well. The Prophet was supported by Khadija during the earlier days. It aligns to how I feel about partnership. A husband's worth does not depend on how much he earns, it is beyond what he can provide materially. It is also how he provides emotionally. Although, the same thing also applies for a wife. I wonder what the concept of *qawwam* means though.<br>
<br>
In a marriage, a lot of things happen in turn. During pregnancy, a husband can't biologically replace a wife's role. Thus, a husband must support his wife. When life gets hard or when something unexpected happens to a husband, a wife should be ready to take her turn. I think a marriage is more nuanced than the husband becoming a provider and the wife being a homemaker. A father needs to be emotionally and physically present for children as well. A lack of father love could negatively affect children in so many ways.<br>
<br>
However, we love reduction, don't we? We like to assign roles, because it is easy. We like to label things because our brain works in simplicity. Huh, the theory of evolution, is it?<br>
<br>
Perhaps modern life encourages us to believe we should be able to solve everything by ourselves, rather than learning to depend on one another. And as an only child, I am overly used to do everything by myself, to be told that I need to be capable because my parents only have me during their old days. Before I went to therapy, I used to think that my purpose is this world is just to care for my parents, not to live my own life. I guess therapy did put a lot of unnecessary weights off my shoulders.<br>
<br>
A partnership, a husband and a wife, I wonder how are they supposed to live together exactly? I guess I need to open the Qur'an again.
